### The research, in simple terms

The paper builds on prior work by my supervisor, Professor Lisa Zhang, which explored **why students are motivated to study machine learning**. Our question was slightly different: if motivation matters so much, do *machine learning textbooks*, especially their introductory chapters, actually align with what students care about?

Textbooks are a surprisingly underexplored medium in computer science and ML education. For a field growing as fast as ML, it felt important to ask whether the materials students first encounter reflect their real interests and motivations.

[(Read the paper in detail here!)](https://underline.io/events/501/sessions/21716/lecture/139089-analysis-of-motivations-in-machine-learning-textbooks)

I cared about this topic personally. I am deeply interested in ML, but I also remember what it felt like to be a beginner, and in many ways, I still am one. Understanding whether the field and its learning resources truly resonate with students felt important, especially as ML becomes more central across disciplines.

The research process itself was difficult. This kind of study hasn‚Äôt been done much before, and qualitative analysis was new territory for me. I spent a long time reading and coding the introductions of ten different ML textbooks, which meant not only reading carefully, but also retaining, organizing, and making sense of a lot of information. One moment that really stood out was receiving recognition from my supervisor for my research methodology. Having her guidance throughout the process made a huge difference, and it helped me grow more confident in my work.
